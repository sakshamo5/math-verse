About Carl Friedrich Gauss

Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777-1855) was a German mathematician known for his profound contributions to number theory, statistics, analysis, differential geometry, and linear algebra.

He displayed extraordinary talent from a young age, famously correcting his father’s arithmetic at just three years old. His groundbreaking work spanned number theory, algebra, statistics, analysis, differential geometry, geophysics, and astronomy. His book Disquisitiones Arithmeticae (1801) laid the foundation for modern number theory. Gauss also made significant contributions to magnetism, optics, and astronomy, including predicting the orbit of the dwarf planet Ceres. Renowned for his precision and depth, Gauss remained dedicated to scientific rigor until his death on February 23, 1855. His legacy endures through numerous theorems, concepts, and mathematical tools bearing his name, such as the Gaussian distribution and Gauss's law.
